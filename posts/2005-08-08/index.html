    The victor and the defeated: 60 years later

    Posted at — Aug 8, 2005

    Taiwan News

    The victor and the defeated: 60 years later by James Wang

    On the eve of the end of the Second World War, one recently released report come to my mind. It is unrelated to the war, but it reminds us how much has changed in East Asia since the end of the war.

    In a report to the Congress on the military power of the PRC, the Petagon states that China’s modernization of its military, over the long run, could pose a credible threat to other modern militaries operating in the region.

    If we read the report together with the outrageous statement made by PLA Gen. Chu Chengwu that PRC should use nuclear weapons against the U.S. in case the U.S. intervenes to defend Taiwan, we can see clearly who the demon is.

    The Pentagon report states the obvious trend foreseen almost 60 years ago by the great warrior of the Pacific region, Gen. Douglas MacArthur, and, in a sense, Generalissimo Chiang Kai-shek.

    Gen. MacArthur and Gimo Chiang shared strong anit-communism ideology and strategy to defeat the communist China before it rises to threat the free world. But their similarity stopped there. In terms of statemanship and dedication to democracy and freedom, Gimo Chiang is not MacArthur.

    Both Gen. MacArthur and Gimo Chiang played an important role in the post war era. Gen. MacArthur occupied and administered the defeated Japan while Gimo Chiang occupied and administered Formosa and Pescadores, which were parts of territories of the defeated Japan, pending the conclusion of peace treaty.

    Gen. MacArthur arrived in Tokyo as Japanese lined up the streets with their backs toward him as a sign of defiance. They expected humiliation, starvations and deprivations as a result of defeat and occupations by the victor and conqueor.

    But Gen. MacArthur administered Japan as a democratic reformer, who directed the adoption of a new, mordern, democratic constitution which transfers sovereignty from the Emperor to the Japanese people and makes Japan give up war as a means in solving international disputes.

    He gave Japanese women voting rights and the farmers the land they deserve.

    He brought liberal education to Japan to substitute for its ultranationalistic and militaristic indoctrinations.

    And he began rebuilding Japan from the ruins and showed her way to economic prosperity.

    When he was dismissed by President Harry Truman over policy disputes, Japanese bursted into tears in bidding him goodbye.

    Prime Minister Yoshida’s broadcast for the nation said it well, “The accomplishments of Gen. MacArthur in the interest of our country are one of the marvels of history. It is he who has salvaged our nation from post-surrender confusion and prostration, and steered the country on the road of recovery and recontruction. It is he who has firmly planted democracy in all sements of our society…I have no words to convey the regret of our nation to see him leave.”

    Unfortunately, Gimo Chiang and his general’s occupation of Taiwan was a different story. Gimo Chiang’s troops arrived in Formosa as “liberator,” and welcome as “compatriots” at the early statage.

    But the KMT occupying forces behaved as victor and conqueror. They were not eager to rebuild Taiwan or make necessary reforms but take the advantage of confusion to benefit themselves.

    Taiwanese exepcted to enjoy better treatment by the occupation administration of “mother land,” but what they got were incompetence, mismanagement and corruption of the Chinese officials. Taiwanese were denied participations in the government and excluded from significant areas of trade and commerce.

    The occupation forces had no idea what is democracy and what is reform. What they understood was to get rich by corruptions. The worsening of living conditions led to island-wide uprising on Feb. 28, 1947, within merely 16 months of occupation. A generation of elite Taiwanese were wiped out in the massive purges and murders.

    Furthermore, the government of KMT annexed Taiwan as a province of China even before peace treaty with Japan was negotiated. It took measures to tranform Taiwnese into “Chinese” and carried out Chinese nationalism, and later anticommunism, propaganda in place of Japnaese indoctrination in nationalism and militarism.

    The KMT government, which was defeated by the Chinese communists, exiled to Taiwan and imposed on Taiwan a constitution that had never been implemented in China and had nothing to do with Taiwan.

    The U.S. concluded its occupation of Japan in seven years, the KMT government continued to occupied Taiwan until year 2000 when they were voted out by the people of Taiwan in the democratic process which had been finally established in 1996.

    Japan took a short cut while Taiwan took a torturous route, but both have become peace-loving democracies.

    The irony is that China emerged as a “victor” after sufferring long conflicts with Japan. The communists replaced the nationalists as the rulers of China and combined communism with nationalism in totalitarian system.

    China was the country that fought against the United Nations and the U.S. forces in the Korea war. It was the one fighting the U.S. with proxy in Viet Nam.

    With the disintegraion of Soviet Union, China became the only victory country that threaten peace and stability. It challenges the status quo that has existed for 60 years.

    China is rising, but without political reform and democracy, it could only rise as a demon and a threat. China is showing it in words and deeds.

    James Wang is a Washington-based journalist.
