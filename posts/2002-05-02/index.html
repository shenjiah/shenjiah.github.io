    San Francisco Peace Treaty Revisited

    Posted at — May 2, 2002

    For Taipei Times

    San Francisco Peace Treaty Revisited by James Wang

    When former president Lee Teng-hui embraced the idea that “sovereignty resides in the people,” his statement didn’t raise many eyebrows. Almost everyone took it for granted because it is the ABC’s of Democarcy and it was stipulated in Article 2 of the Constitution.

    And, when George W. Bush, President of the United States, insisted that the resolution of the disputes between Taiwan and the People’s Republic of Chinashould have the consent of the people of Taiwan, he seemed also talking along the line of Democracy.

    But, putting together, these statements were milestones in the evolution of Taiwan’s legal status.

    Students of U.S. policies toward China and Taiwan are well aware of late president Harry Truman’s statement in 1950 announcing that the status of Taiwan remained to be determined. But they didn’t pay much attention to the fact that, technically, the San Francisco Peace Treaty with Japan failed to settle the sovereignty issue of Taiwan.

    “No provision has been made either in the San Francisco Treaty or the Sino-Japanese Treaty as to the future of Taiwan and Penghu,” late foreign minister George Yeh explained to the Legislative Yuan.

    “In fact, we are controlling them now, and undoubtly they constitute a part of our territories. However, the delicate international situation make it that they do not belong to us. Under the circumstances, Japan has no right to transfer Formosa and the Pescadores to us. Nor can we accept such a transfer from Japan even if she so wishes,” Goerge Yeh added.

    This was one of the statements cited by U. S. State Department legal experts to support their position that from legal point of views the sovereignty of Taiwan remained undetermined.

    In the declassified State Department files, I found two memoranda with regard to the legal status of Taiwan, one was done in 1961 by Mr. John Czyzak, legal adiviser of the Far East Affairs Bureau; another was a rewritting of Mr. Czyzak’s study in non-technical language by Robert Starr in 1971.

    According to Mr. Czyzak’s study, four legal theories have been advanced concerning the status of Taiwan and the Pescadores:

    1. Sovereignty over the islands has not been finally determined.

    2. The islands belong to the Republic of China.

    3. The islands belong to China.

    4. The islands now form a condominium belong either to the victorious powers of the Second World War or to the parties to the Japanese Peace Treaty.

    After a lengthy anaysis, Mr. Czyzak concluded that “the most tenable theory regarding the status of Formosa and the Pescadores is that sovereignty over the islands has not been finally determined.” The conclusion was restated in Mr. Starr’s memorandum in 1971 and accepted as legal and political positions of the United States.

    In fact, Mr. Charles Bray, spokeman of the State Department, repeated this position on April 28, 1971. And in preparartions for the normalization of relations with PRC, Mr. John Stevenson, legal adviser of the State Department, wrote a memorandum to Mr. Marshall Green, Assistant Secretary of State, on Nov. 12, 1971 that “since the 1952 Japanese Peace Treaty, the United States has taken the position that status of Taiwan is undetermined, subject to some future international resolution. That position has been stated publicly from time to time.”

    According to Mr. Stevenson, such position has formed an important legal basis for multual defense treaty with Taiwan and “a potential legal basis for Taiwan Independence if that should ever become a realitic possibility.”

    Mr. Steveson didn’t realize that Dr. Henry A. Kissinger, National Security Adviser to President Richard M. Nixon, had already assured Chinese premier Chou En-lai during his secret visit to Beijing that the U. S. government would not repeat Mr. Bray’s statement again.

    Mr. Nixon made the same promise in conversations with Chou in 1972:" There will be no more statement made, if I can control our bureaucracy, to the effect that the status of Taiwan is undetermined."

    Even Mr. Nixon and his successors have successfully silenced the bureaucracy on this issue, they can not deny the validity of Mr. Czyzak’s legal analysis.

    According to Mr. Czyzak, sovereignty of Taiwan and Penghu in later date might belong to a) part of a single state of China; b)part of the state of the Republic of China as it has been left as a result of the Chinese civil war; c) a new state.

    “The decision can be made through agreement by victorious countries of World War II or by agreement by parties to Peace Treaty, through a plebicite or through international recognition of the continued occupation by an authority claiming full sovereignty,” Mr. Czyzak wrote.

    That is why Mr. Lee’s statement of “sovereignty residing in the people” was a milestone in the evolution of Taiwan’s legal status. Since Republic of China has been “Taiwanized” through democratic processes, even if it is not a new state, it is a separate state from the PRC and its claims of sovereignty over Taiwan is the most tenable.

    In that sense it is especially heartened to hear President Goerge W. Bush solemnly stating that the resolution of disputes between Taiwan and the PRC should have the consent of the people in Taiwan. This is the closest thing he can say short of recognizing Taiwan as a sovereign state.

    Mr. Bush is in sync with Mr. Lee’s advocate that sovereignty resides in the people.

    James Wang is a Washington-based journalist.
