    Henry Kissinger Forgets What He said

    Posted at — Jun 27, 2005

    Taipei Times

    Henry Kissinger Forgets What He said by James Wang

    Dr. Henry A. Kissinger, former U.S. secretary of state, is a man known for his craftiness in the nuance of diplomatic language. But, for some reasons, his grips of the skill seems beginning to rust, as it showed in his article, China: Containment Won’t Work, published in the Washington Post on June 13, 2005.

    Dr. Kissinger stated in that article that “almost all countries – and all major ones – have recognized China’s claim that Taiwan is part of China. So have seven American presidents of both parties –none more emphatically than George W. Bush.” He misstated the facts and abandoned the language he deliberately chose in the Shanghai Communique and used in other two joint communiques between the United States and China.

    The fact is that,in the Shanghai Coomuniques, the United States “acknowledges” that all Chinese on either side of the Taiwan Strait maintain there is but one China and that Taiwan is part of China. The United States does not challenge that position. And, in the joint communique announcing the establishment of diplomatic relations, the Government of the United States also “acknowledges” the Chinese position that there is but one China and Taiwan is part of China.

    Having found that the Chinese text translated “acknowledge” as “cheng-ren,” the American side emphasized that to acknowledge is different from to recognize (Cheng-ren) and the Unted States holds the English text as official one. Now Dr. Kissinger forgot what the U.S. insisted, accepted the language used by the Chinese and made a false statement that the U.S. “recognizes” Chinese claim over Taiwan.

    In dealing with Chinese claims, countries like Japan used different language to avoid recognizing Chinese position. Some countries just “take note of " Chinese position and Japan expressed its “understanding” of Chinese position. To say that all major countries “recognize” Chinese position on this issue is a unforgivable factual error.

    Dr. Kissinger mentioned that the improvement of relations between the U.S. and China has been based on three principles, one of them was “American recognition of the one China principle and opposition to an independent Taiwan.” He should know better or he should double-check with his memorandum of conversations with Chinese leaders. The U. S. has its “One China policy” which is different from so-called “one China principle” asserted by the Chinese.

    From the very beginning of the high level contacts between the U.S. and China in 1971, American officials, including Henry Kissinger and President Richard M. Nixon, have assured Chinese leaders that the U.S. government “does not support Taiwan independent movement.” When President Bill Clinton stated his “three nos” in Shanghai in 1998, he changed the language to the U.S. “does not support Taiwan independent.” He was dishonest, when responded to congressional inquiries, in saying that his assurances had been there since Nixon’s visit to China in 1972.

    Just as their twisting of the words “to acknowledge” into “to recognize,” the Chinese translate “does not support” as “to oppose” in Chinese. Once again Dr. Kissinger forgets the language he crafted at the very beginning and mistakingly uses the stronger language preferred by the Chinese translation.

    As usual, Dr. Kissinger showed strong sympathy for China and accepted China’s defense against outside criticism. He arugued that Chinese military budget is less than 20 percent of America’s and mush less than the combined military budgets of Japan, India and Russia, not to speak of Taiwan’s military modernization support by American decision made in 2001. He preferred to ignore the fact that none of those countries posed any threat to China. It is China that has been building up its military and boasting that their major goal was to deter the U.S. from defending Taiwan agianst China’s ivasion that cause alarm. in the region. Dr. Kissinger just ignore the fact that China is challenging America’s long standing position that the issue of Taiwan Strait should be settled by peaceful means.

    I do not have answer to the question why Dr. Kissinger shifted his use of sensitive languages and adopted Chinese positions. I am puzzled. But to his credits, he honestly admits in his Washington Post article that China is a client of his consulting firm and he spent a week in China in May “much of it as a guest of the government.” That may help us understand his views better.

    James Wang is a Washington-based journalist
