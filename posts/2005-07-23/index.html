    U.S. Should Help Taiwan On the Diplomatic Front

    Posted at — Jul 23, 2005

    Taipei Times

    U.S. Should Help Taiwan On the Diplomatic Front

                                   by James Wang

    Although the U.S. government continues to complain about the lack of transparency in China’s military modernizaton, it is crystal clear that China’s militray build-up poses a seriuos threat to the security of Taiwan and, in the long terms, the U.S. forces in the area.

    China has arrogantly repeated its threats and intimidations through words and deeeds. And the annual report to the U.S. Congress by the Pentagon could not but draw that alarming conclusion.

    To help Taiwan maintain its credible deterrence against China’s use of forces and prevent Taiwan from being involuntarily annexed, the U.S. has approved sale of a package of advanced defensise weapons to Taiwan. This is an important countermeasure for the U.S. and Taiwan to take and it is consistent with the deterrence strategy adopted by both countries to maintain peace and stability in the Taiwan Strait.

    But it is not enough to strengthen Taiwan’s military front for the deterrence strategy to work. As the Pentagon report points out, the cross-Starit military balance appears to be “shifting toward Beijing as a result of China’s sustained economic growth, growing diplomatic leverage, and improvments in the PLA’s military capabilities.” Therefore, it is obvious that merely beefing up defense forces is inadquate to address the increasing imbalace of power between Taiwan and China.

    To maintain the status quo and insure peaceful resolution of disputes between the two sides, the U.S. should also help Taiwan on the diplomatic front.

    When the U.S. normalized its relations with China in 1979, American officials argued that the U.S. should continue to sell defensive weapons to Taiwan and make Taiwan feel secure and confident to begin dialogue with China. But the ruling authoritarian regime of KMT, claiming as representing all China, refused even to “contact” with PRC.

    The irony is that the U.S. critized KMT regime’s suppression of human rights and pushed for democratization in Taiwan, but when Taiwan achieved its democratization, elected a truely representative government, and gave up its claims of sovereignty over China, the U.S. continued to ignore the fact that Taiwan and China are “mutually unsubordinated” separate states. The U.S. wants to maintain “status quo” in the Taiwan Strait area but refuses to recognize a democratic government’s position that the status quo of Taiwan is a sovereign country with official name as Republic of China.

    The democratization of Taiwan did not win recognization from the U.S. and other countries. On the contrary, it opened up the political process for China to exploit under its united front stategy. By refusing to conduct dialogue with Taiwan government on equal footing on the one hand, and welcoming KMT and other politicians who earnestly curry favour with their old nenemy under its terms on the other, China tries to damage domestic sovereignty of DPP government.

    China’s strategy was to threat Taiwan with military forces and isolate Taiwan with diplomatic leverage, with the goal to annex Taiwan and change the status quo of regional balnace of power in its favour.

    For Taiwan to deal with such difficult situation and maintain its status as a democratic sovereign country, it needs not only military deterrence forces but also diplomatic recognitions. The U.S. and the international community can not and should not deprive Taiwan of its diplomatic status to fight against an involuntary “unification.”

    As international lawyers would argue that there is nothing standing in the way for two sovereign states to merge peacefully as one if they wish, there is no reason for the U.S. government and international community to deny diplomatic recognitions to Taiwan that it deserves.

    With international recognition, Taiwan would feel more secure and confident to deal with China. It may also eliminate the pressure on the Taiwan government to change its official name or revise the constitution in order to win international recognition.

    With its economic growth and military modernization, China’s influence in the Asia-Pacific area is increasing. The U.S. is right to welcome a peacful and prosperous China as a responsible member of international community. But it is wrong to accommodate a communist regime’s goal and strategy and deny the necessary and peaceful means for the 23 million people of Taiwan to defend their independent status and freedom. This policy is wrong and it should not statnd.

    James Wang is a Washington-based journalist.
