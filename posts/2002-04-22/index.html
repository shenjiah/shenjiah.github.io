    Mr. Lien's Political Judgment In Question

    Posted at — Apr 22, 2002

    For Taipei Times

    Mr. Lien’s Political Judgment In Question by James Wang

    At a time when the Middle east was in crisis, the Bush Administration was under fire for its inaction in seeking for peace, and the situation in the Taiwan Strait was relatively quiet, it is hard to explain why Mr. Lien Chan, Chairman of the KMT, took a trip to Washington, D.C.

    Mr. Lien’s visit generated a brief report in the Washington Times, which lead the story by quoting Mr. Lien as predicting a Nationalists comeback. But I can hardly recall any opposition leader failed to make such predictions. It is not news and it doesn’t make sense to travel half the distance of the globe to make such prediction.

    I tend to believe that Mr. Lien’s visit was hurriedly arranged to pre-empt the previously scheduled visit of former president Lee Teng-hui in late May, which had since been cancelled. He wanted to address the National Press Club ahead of Mr. Lee and spread messages that may serve to blunt Mr. Lee’s advocates for Taiwan’s security and participations in international communities.

    But he was not well prepared and not effective. Absent from Mr. Lien’s messages were the achievements of democracy in Taiwan, the constant threat to Taiwan’s security by the People’s Republic of China, the need for Taiwan to participate in international communities and the hiatus of dialogue between Taiwan and PRC due to PRC’s insistence on Taiwan’s acceptance of its pre-condition.

    On the contrary, he talked about the woes of Taiwan’s economy since President Chen Sui-bian took office two years ago. He blamed on Chen’s administration for the deadlock between Taiwan and PRC. He charged that the DPP government has adopted Taiwan Independence policies and even accused the government of purposely maintaining tension in the Taiwan Strait in order to win support in elections.

    Mr. Lien reiterated the so-called “One China, yes, but not yet” formula that was promoted by KMT in the early 90’s. Of course, he gave a laundry list of KMT victories in local elections since it debacles in the Presidential election in the year 2000 and the parlimentary election in 2001 and predicted a KMT comeback in the future.

    Talking to a foreign audience in a foreign countries on such sensitive topics, Mr. Lien violated one cardinal rule of politics: there is one President at a time and you don’t critize him while you are overseas. Even worse, he seemed singing from the same sheet of music with Beijing. A Chinese reporter loved his “One China” message so much that he asked if Mr. Lien is willing to visit China to promote “One China” principle. For sure, he left out Mr. Lien’s “but” clause.

    Mr. Lien said that the “One China, yes, but not yet” formula was a classical face-saving measure for PRC. But I don’t think it is a meaningless measure for PRC or a harmless concession from Taiwan. It would give PRC a legal base to claim its sovereignty over Taiwan. It is a fatal cost for Taiwan. And Mr. Lien didn’t even bother to state that any change of Taiwan’s status quo should follow its democratic processes.

    One message Mr. Lien volunteered and emphasized was that the sentiment of Taiwanese versus mainlanders no longer exists. It struck me as odd. I don’t believe he is so out of touch with the reality in Taiwan and ignorant of different voting patterens and certain policy orientations. If no such sentiment existed since the 1960’s, as Mr, Lien said, then why the KMT tickets in the Presidential elections since 1978 have always included on mainlander and one Taiwanese?

    On the issue of so-called “national reunification”, it is undeniable that the mainlanders are more inclined to favor that goal while Taiwanese more inclined to oppose. Mr. Lien may think it unfortunate, but is is natural in a democratic society. Any politician who fails to face this reality is not expected to do well in Taiwan’s politics. That was why KMT won in 1996 when Mr. Lee Teng-hui was on top of the ticket and KMT lost badly in 2000 when Mr. Lien was the presidential candidate.

    An opposition leader should have a shrewd judgment on what, when and where to attack the government and his opponents. By choosing to attack President Chen on China policy and to mock his mentor Mr. Lee’s political influence as “passe” and “irrelevant” while he was in the United States, Mr. Lien showed his plitical judgment has more room for improvement.

    James Wang is a Washington-based journalist.
