    Calling a spade a spade

    Posted at — Apr 14, 2002

    Calling a spade a spade James C. Wang

    Referring to Taiwan as “The Republic of Taiwan,” President George W. Bush of the United States caused an uproar in China and a ripple in Taiwan.

    As can be expected, Beijing asked for clarification and government-controlled media expressed their dismay and indignation. That is routine business. No matter whether the remark is intentional or a slip of tongue, Beijing has no choice but to protest and put their position on the record.

    As a matter of necessity, China loads protests to the United States whenever it perceives any American action as violation of its so-called “One China"principle. China wants to build up its case that Taiwan is “part of China.” Even though Taiwan has been a separate country for more than half a century, China will do whatever it can to weaken Taiwan’s separate identity and make its claim of sovereignty over Taiwan more credible.

    China would not admit it, but it is obvious that it has no legal base to claim sovereignty over Taiwan except insisting that Taiwan and international community accept its “One China” principle. Taiwan as a democracy can not and should not fall into this trap. Neither should the United Satets.

    In contrast to the one-voice negative reaction in China, the reactions in the democratic Taiwan were as usual more divided. The pro-independence or pro-status quo forever majority deeply appreciated to hear President Bush called a spade a spade. The small but vocal pro-unification mainlanders shared their outrage with the Chinese on the mainland against President Bush and presumed that President Bush misspoke.

    I can understand China’s reaction because it is in their interests. But I am puzzled by the reaction of the tiny pro-unification group in Taiwan who were part of the staunch anti-commuinists ruling authorities in the martial law era. Now they prefer to live in Taiwan but want to see Taiwan under China’s control!

    To be sure, the constitutional name of Taiwan is not the Republic of Taiwan. But Taiwan is a well-known common name of the area across the Taiwan Straits independent from China and politically Taiwan is a Republic. What else you can find a name better fit to identify Taiwan in international community than the Republic of Taiwan?

    After more than half a century of political evolution, Republic of China has been “Taiwanized,” no longer claiming or being recognized as government of China. The battle over China representation was over in 1971 when the Republic of China was soundly defeated and expelled from the United Nations. To continue to claim that ROC represents China should be considered as “provocative” to China and crazy. The only way for Taiwan to survive as an independent state is to build up its separate identity through democratic process.

    When the mainlander-dominated KMT was in power, the authorities refused to adapt a new separate identity because it could cost them the legal base, however false it was, to rule Taiwan. Legally speaking the sovereignty of Taiwan should belong to either the people of Taiwan or China. No wonder Chiang Kai-shek would rather have jade broken than an earthern tile intact.

    Chiang’s hardlines were not necessarily shared by all the ruling mainlanders. According to recently declassified diplomatic files, U.S. Ambassador Watlter McConaughy reported to Washington after the U.N. expulsion in 1971 that H. K. Yang, then vice foreign minister, had told him that for Taiwan to survive it is necessary to declare that “the government of Taiwan is entirely separate and apart from the government on the mainland and that henceforth the government here will have nothing to do with the mainland.”

    H. K. Yang said, according to Amb. McConaughy, the declaration should prescribe a new designation for the government, namely “The Chinese Republis of Taiwan.” It would be stipulated that the term Chinese did not have any political connotation but was used merely as a generic term stemming from the Chinese ethnic origin of the population in Taiwan. It would be used in a way similar to the manner in which the various Arab countries use “Arab” in their official government titles.

    Of course, it is now history. But it showed that H. K. Yang understood that technically the legal status of Taiwan remained to be determined. For ROC to have legal case to rule Taiwan as a sovereign state, it has to give up its claims of representing all China, which had been a lost cause, and become government of Taiwan.

    In that sense, President Bush almost cited the same name for Taiwan as suggested by H. K. Yang some 30 years ago. They all pointed to the fact that Taiwan is a separate state. Through demoractic process, which is consistent with the

    U.N. charter and international covenants on human rights, the people of Taiwan have made it clear that Taiwan is a sovereign state. Constitutionally it is still The Republic of China, but it is “entirely separate and apart” from the government on mainland and has nothing to do with mainland.

    Eventually Taiwan may be able to call a spade a spade and every one would be proud to be citizen of the Republic of Taiwan, which would co-exist peacefully with all its neighbors, including the People’s Republic of China.

    (The writer is a Washington-based journalist)
